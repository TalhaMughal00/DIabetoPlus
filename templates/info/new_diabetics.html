What is Diabetes?

Diabetes is a chronic health condition that affects how your body turns food into energy, particularly in the way it processes blood sugar (glucose). Blood sugar is the main source of energy for the cells, and it comes from the food you eat. The hormone insulin, produced by the pancreas, helps glucose enter the cells for energy. In diabetes, either the body doesn’t produce enough insulin, can’t use insulin effectively, or both, leading to high blood sugar levels over time.

Blood Sugar

Blood Sugar (Glucose) is the primary sugar found in your blood, and it’s the main source of energy for your body’s cells. Blood sugar comes from the food you eat, particularly carbohydrates, and it circulates through your bloodstream, delivering energy to cells for immediate use or storing it for later.

Insulin

Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas. It helps glucose enter the cells for energy. Insulin is released from the cells when your blood sugar levels are high. Your body uses insulin to help glucose leave the cells, and it then uses it to replenish the blood sugar levels. Insulin can also help you lose weight by reducing the need for glucose intake.

Symptoms

Diabetes symptoms can include increased thirst, frequent urination, increased hunger, fatigue, and unexplained weight loss. Other signs are blurred vision, slow-healing sores, frequent infections, and tingling in the hands or feet. Type 1 symptoms often appear quickly and are severe, while type 2 symptoms may be milder and develop slowly. Gestational diabetes typically has no noticeable symptoms but is identified through screening.

Diagnosis

Diabetes is diagnosed by testing for blood sugar levels. If your blood sugar level is consistently high or uncontrolled, you may be diagnosed with diabetes. If you have any of the symptoms listed above, it may be a sign that you have diabetes. If you're unsure, consult a healthcare professional.

Type 1

Type 1 Diabetes is a chronic autoimmune condition where the immune system attacks insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas, resulting in little or no insulin production. It typically develops in children and young adults, although it can occur at any age. Symptoms include increased thirst, frequent urination, extreme hunger, weight loss, fatigue, and blurred vision. Diagnosis is made through blood tests measuring blood sugar levels. Management requires lifelong insulin therapy, regular blood sugar monitoring, a balanced diet, and exercise to maintain blood sugar within target ranges. If unmanaged, Type 1 diabetes can lead to serious short-term complications like hypoglycemia and long-term complications affecting the heart, kidneys, eyes, and nerves.

Type 2

Type 2 Diabetes is a chronic condition where the body resists insulin or doesn’t produce enough, resulting in high glucose levels. Commonly seen in adults and increasingly in children due to obesity, its key risk factors include genetics, poor diet, and inactivity. Symptoms may include increased thirst, frequent urination, hunger, fatigue, and blurred vision. Diagnosis is through blood tests like fasting blood sugar and A1C. Management focuses on lifestyle changes, such as a healthy diet and exercise, along with medications if needed. Regular monitoring is essential to prevent complications like heart disease and kidney damage.

Gestational Diabetes

Gestational Diabetes occurs during pregnancy when the body cannot produce enough insulin, leading to elevated blood sugar levels. Common in women with risk factors like obesity or a family history of diabetes, it often has no clear symptoms and is diagnosed through screening tests between the 24th and 28th weeks. Management involves a healthy diet, regular exercise, and monitoring blood sugar levels, with insulin or oral medications if needed. If unmanaged, it can result in complications for both mother and baby, including a higher risk of type 2 diabetes and delivery issues.

Other Types

In addition to Type 1, Type 2, and gestational diabetes, several other types exist. Prediabetes is a condition where blood sugar levels are elevated but not high enough for a diabetes diagnosis, often leading to Type 2 diabetes. Maturity Onset Diabetes of the Young (MODY) is a rare genetic form that typically occurs in adolescents or young adults, often misdiagnosed as Type 1 or Type 2. Latent Autoimmune Diabetes in Adults (LADA) is a slowly progressing autoimmune diabetes in adults, resembling both Type 1 and Type 2. Secondary diabetes arises from other medical conditions or medications, such as Cushing's syndrome or corticosteroid use. Neonatal diabetes occurs in infants within the first six months, caused by genetic mutations, and can be either transient or permanent. Lastly, Type 3c diabetes develops due to pancreatic diseases like pancreatitis or cancer. Each type has unique causes and management strategies, highlighting the need for accurate diagnosis and treatment.

Type 1 Diabetes

The exact cause of Type 1 Diabetes is not fully understood, but it is primarily an autoimmune condition where the immune system mistakenly attacks and destroys the insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas. This destruction leads to little or no insulin production, resulting in elevated blood sugar levels. Genetic factors play a significant role, as individuals with a family history of Type 1 diabetes are at higher risk. Additionally, environmental triggers, such as viral infections, may contribute to the onset of the disease, prompting the immune response that damages the pancreas. Overall, it is a complex interplay of genetic predisposition and environmental factors that leads to Type 1 diabetes.

Type 2 Diabetes

The primary causes of Type 2 Diabetes involve a combination of genetic, lifestyle, and environmental factors. Insulin resistance, where the body's cells do not respond effectively to insulin, is a key feature of this condition. This resistance can develop due to factors such as obesity, physical inactivity, and poor dietary habits, which contribute to excessive fat accumulation, particularly around the abdomen. Additionally, genetics play a significant role, as having a family history of diabetes increases the risk. Other contributing factors include age, with the risk rising as individuals get older, and certain medical conditions like hypertension or high cholesterol. Overall, Type 2 diabetes results from a complex interplay of lifestyle choices and genetic susceptibility, leading to impaired insulin function and elevated blood sugar levels.

Treatment of Diabetes

Insulin given by injection is the central treatment for type 1 diabetes. There are fast acting and long acting insulin formulations. It is important to understand when to use these different formulations and the concepts behind choosing the right insulin dose. There is one other injected medication for type 1 diabetes, Symlin, that may be given in addition to insulin.

Insulin and its Types

Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas that plays a crucial role in regulating blood sugar (glucose) levels. It allows cells to absorb glucose from the bloodstream for energy or storage, thereby lowering blood sugar levels. Insulin also helps control how the body stores and uses carbohydrates, fats, and proteins.

  • Rapid-acting insulin: This type begins to work within 15 minutes and lasts for about 3 to 5 hours. It is typically used to manage blood sugar spikes after meals. Examples include insulin lispro (Humalog) and insulin aspart (NovoLog).
  • Short-acting insulin:Also known as regular insulin, it starts to work within 30 minutes and lasts about 6 to 8 hours. It can be used for meal coverage as well. Examples include regular insulin (Humulin R, Novolin R).
  • Intermediate-acting insulin:This type takes about 2 to 4 hours to start working and lasts around 12 to 18 hours. It is often used for basal insulin coverage. An example is insulin NPH (Humulin N, Novolin N).
  • Long-acting insulin:It begins to work several hours after injection and provides a steady release of insulin over 24 hours or more. It is used for basal insulin needs. Examples include insulin glargine (Lantus, Toujeo) and insulin detemir (Levemir).
  • Ultra-long-acting insulin:This type has an even longer duration of action, lasting over 24 hours. It is designed to provide consistent insulin levels throughout the day and night. An example is insulin degludec (Tresiba).
  • Pre-mixed insulin:This combines different types of insulin, usually a rapid-acting and an intermediate-acting insulin, to provide both mealtime and basal coverage in one injection. Examples include 70/30 (70% NPH and 30% regular) formulations.

Each type of insulin serves different purposes and is used based on individual needs, lifestyle, and the type of diabetes being treated. Proper management of insulin therapy is crucial for maintaining stable blood sugar levels and preventing complications.

Pills

In addition to insulin, several oral medications are available to manage blood sugar levels in individuals with Type 2 Diabetes. Metformin is the first-line treatment, improving insulin sensitivity and reducing glucose production in the liver. Sulfonylureas and meglitinides stimulate the pancreas to release more insulin, while DPP-4 inhibitors increase insulin production and decrease glucose output in response to meals. GLP-1 receptor agonists enhance insulin secretion and promote satiety, though some are injectable. SGLT2 inhibitors prevent glucose reabsorption in the kidneys, leading to increased glucose excretion in urine. Lastly, thiazolidinediones (TZDs) improve insulin sensitivity in muscle and fat tissues. Each medication has unique mechanisms, benefits, and side effects, necessitating personalized treatment plans in consultation with healthcare providers.

When the Blood Sugar is Uncontrolled

When your blood sugar is too high or too low, you need to understand the symptoms and what to do. Some situations require urgent medical attention.

  • Low Blood Sugars
  • High Blood Sugars

When the Blood Sugar is Uncontrolled

When blood sugar levels are not controlled, it can lead to both short-term and long-term complications. In the short term, persistently high blood sugar can cause symptoms like increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, and blurred vision. If left untreated, it may result in diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), a life-threatening condition characterized by high blood sugar and ketone buildup. Long-term, uncontrolled blood sugar can lead to serious complications such as heart disease, kidney damage, nerve damage (neuropathy), vision problems (including diabetic retinopathy), and an increased risk of infections. Effective management is crucial to prevent these health issues and maintain overall well-being.

Low Blood Sugars

When blood sugar levels drop too low, a condition known as hypoglycemia occurs. Symptoms can include shakiness, sweating, confusion, irritability, rapid heartbeat, dizziness, and hunger. If blood sugar levels continue to fall, it can lead to severe symptoms such as seizures, loss of consciousness, or even coma. Hypoglycemia can be triggered by factors such as taking too much insulin or diabetes medication, skipping meals, excessive exercise, or alcohol consumption. It is important to treat low blood sugar promptly by consuming fast-acting carbohydrates, such as glucose tablets, fruit juice, or sugary snacks, to quickly raise blood sugar levels and prevent serious complications.

High Blood Sugars

When blood sugar levels are too high, a condition called hyperglycemia occurs. Symptoms can include increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blurred vision, and headache. If hyperglycemia persists and is not managed, it can lead to serious complications, such as diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) in Type 1 diabetes, characterized by high blood sugar and ketone buildup, which can be life-threatening. In Type 2 diabetes, prolonged high blood sugar can result in a hyperglycemic hyperosmolar state (HHS), also dangerous and requiring urgent medical attention. Chronic hyperglycemia can cause long-term complications, including damage to the heart, kidneys, nerves, and eyes. Effective management through medication, lifestyle changes, and regular monitoring is essential to prevent and treat high blood sugar levels.
