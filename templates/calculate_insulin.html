Insulin Sensitivity vs. Resistance:

Insulin sensitivity means your body responds well to insulin, so you may require a lower dose. Insulin resistance means your body doesnâ€™t use insulin as effectively, so you may need a higher dose. Causes of insulin resistance can include:

  • Obesity: Excess body fat, especially around the abdomen, can lead to increased insulin resistance.
  • Physical Inactivity: A sedentary lifestyle can contribute to insulin resistance.
  • Unhealthy Diet: Diets high in refined carbohydrates, sugars, and unhealthy fats can affect insulin sensitivity.
  • Hormonal Changes: Conditions such as polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) can affect hormone levels and lead to insulin resistance.
  • Chronic Stress: High levels of stress can increase cortisol levels, which may lead to insulin resistance.
  • Sleep Disorders: Conditions like sleep apnea can disrupt hormone balance and contribute to insulin resistance.
Total Daily Dosage (TDD):

Your TDD is the total amount of insulin you need in one day. This includes both your basal insulin (long-acting) and bolus insulin (mealtime insulin).

Insulin Correction Factor:

To correct high blood sugar, the 1800 Rule is often used: divide 1800 by your TDD to determine how much your blood sugar will drop per unit of insulin. Adjust accordingly if you're insulin-resistant or insulin-sensitive.

Important:

Always consult your healthcare provider before making any changes to your insulin dosage.
